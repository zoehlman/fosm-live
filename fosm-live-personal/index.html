🎓
Why three pieces, not one
Most people set a money goal as one number — "I want to make $X." That number is usually either too small (and stops them growing) or too big (and feels impossible). The three-piece method is honest: the daily life you want, the experiences you want, and the regular life you already have. Each piece is concrete. Add them up and you get the real number — not a wish, a calculation.

Why "the gap" matters more than the number itself
Most people stare at their income and feel either grateful or guilty. Neither moves anything. The gap is different — it's a problem with a solution. Once you see the exact number standing between you and your dream life, every dollar you earn, every dollar you spend, every dollar you invest is doing one of four things to that gap. Making it smaller, making it bigger, or standing still. The four lenses below are the only four ways the gap actually closes.
The four lenses · how to close the gap
1
Sell more
Increase income. Negotiate the raise. Add the side income. Build the business. Charge what you're worth. Highest leverage of the four — income has no ceiling.
2
Spend less
Reduce expenses. Cut what doesn't matter. Keep what does. Fastest of the four — every $1 saved is $1 you don't need to earn.
3
Pay less in tax
Legally optimize tax structure. Entity choice, retirement contributions, depreciation, charitable strategy. Often overlooked. Talk to a CPA who teaches this.
4
Buy income-producing assets
Real estate. Dividend stocks. Businesses. Royalties. Money that arrives without your time. The endgame — the path to never working again.

📈 Income Statement
What you earned and what you spent. The first of the three financial statements. Pulled live from your Google Sheet — every income source, every expense category, totaled by owner. Use the pills below to slice by His, Hers, Joint, or Combined.
🎓
What an Income Statement actually shows
The Income Statement is a flow — money moving through your life over a period of time (this year, year-to-date). Two halves: income at the top (everything that came in), and expenses below (everything that went out). The bottom line is the difference. When the bottom is positive, you grew. When it's negative, you shrunk. It's the first statement to read because it tells you whether the period was a building period or a using-up period. Pair it with the Cash Flow Statement (where the money actually went) and the Balance Sheet (what's left at the end) to get the full picture.

💸 Cash Flow Statement
Where the money actually went. Three buckets — Operations (your daily life), Investing (assets bought and sold), Financing (debt taken on or paid down). Add them up and you get the net change in cash for the period.
🎓
Why Cash Flow is different from Income Statement
The Income Statement says "you earned $X and spent $Y, net was $Z." The Cash Flow Statement asks a sharper question: where did the money actually move? Three lenses: Operations (your day-to-day life — same as the Income Statement bottom line), Investing (did you buy assets, sell assets?), and Financing (did you take on debt, pay debt off?). A household with great Income Statement performance can still be cash-poor if Operations is going to debt service. A household with mediocre Income Statement can still be wealth-building if it's funneling money into investments. The Cash Flow Statement reveals the strategy.

⚖️ Balance Sheet
What you own minus what owns you. The third statement — and the only one that compounds across time. Your net worth is the single most important number in personal finance, because it's the only one that tracks whether you're actually building something.
🎓
Why the Balance Sheet matters more than the other two
The Income Statement and Cash Flow Statement are flow statements — they tell you what happened during a period. The Balance Sheet is a snapshot — it tells you where you stand right now. The difference matters: a great month on the Income Statement that doesn't move your Balance Sheet means you spent everything you earned. Years of mediocre Income Statements that build the Balance Sheet by 5% per year still compound into wealth. Net worth growth across time is the truest measure of whether your financial life is working. Watch this number, not your income.

🏛 Asset composition
What kinds of assets make up your wealth — and which ones produce income.
Real estate (rental), taxable investments, and businesses produce income. Cash and primary residence don't. The tilt of this composition reveals how close you are to financial independence.
⚠️ Liability composition
What you owe, by category.
Debt against income-producing assets (rental mortgage, business debt) is leverage — it can grow your wealth. Debt against consumption (credit cards, lifestyle loans) is anti-leverage — it shrinks it. The composition matters more than the total.
